## "Objecto Tractatus, but a different version"

Wittgenstein's *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* opens with one of the most famous lines in philosophy: **"The world is everything that is the case."** From there he builds a picture theory of reality — the idea that language, objects, and facts mirror each other. Reality is made of *atomic facts*, which are arrangements of *objects*, and our propositions about the world are like logical pictures of those arrangements.

The key insight is this: **objects are the fixed, unchanging things. The world is how those objects happen to be arranged at any given moment.**

When someone writes "a different version" of this, they're likely asking: *what if you applied this same logical skeleton to something else?* What if you treated a film, a novel, or even a social system as its own "tractatus" — its own theory of what objects exist and how they relate?

## The Matrix as a Tractatus

The Matrix is almost embarrassingly philosophical in this sense. It asks: **what are the real objects of the world?**

The machines have constructed a false arrangement of objects — a simulation. The "facts" people experience are not true facts. Neo's journey is essentially Wittgensteinian: he has to discard the false picture of reality and confront the actual logical structure underneath.

But here's where it diverges from Wittgenstein — and this might be exactly what the notebook writer noticed. For Wittgenstein, **language cannot say what it cannot picture**. There are things that can only be *shown*, not stated. The Matrix dramatizes this: you cannot be *told* what the Matrix is. You have to see it. That's almost a direct theatrical staging of Wittgenstein's limits of language.

**The Architect** (in *Matrix Reloaded*) is particularly interesting here. He sits surrounded by screens — every possible version of reality — and explains that Neo is not the first, that the whole system has been designed and redesigned. He is literally the author of the tractatus. He defines which objects exist and what their relations are. He builds the world as a logical system.

## Gone with the Wind as a Tractatus

This one is less obvious, which makes it more interesting.

Scarlett O'Hara's entire world is built on a very specific arrangement of objects — land, class, race, femininity, honour, the plantation. These aren't just things she owns. They constitute her *reality*. They are the objects of her world in the Wittgensteinian sense.

The Civil War doesn't just destroy the South. It **destroys the logical arrangement** that made that world coherent. The objects (Tara, the old families, slavery as an institution) either vanish or lose their meaning. Scarlett's famous line — *"I'll think about that tomorrow"* — is actually a refusal to accept that the tractatus has changed. She keeps trying to impose the old logical picture onto a world where it no longer fits.

Rhett Butler, interestingly, is the one character who can **see the structure from outside**. He knows the old world is a failing system before it collapses. He's a kind of anti-Architect — someone who reads the logic of a world without being captured by it.

**Ashley Wilkes** represents the tragedy of someone whose entire identity is built into the old tractatus. When it collapses, he has nothing. He cannot form new propositions because his objects no longer exist.

## "The Architects" as a concept

Across both works, there's a figure — sometimes literal, sometimes implied — who **designs the system of objects that everyone else lives inside**.

- In The Matrix, it's the Architect (and before him, the Oracle in a different way)
- In Gone with the Wind, it's the antebellum social order itself — no single person, but a collective architecture of rules about what objects matter and how they relate
- In Wittgenstein, it's *logic itself* — the hidden structure that any possible world must obey

The notebook writer may be trying to identify a **shared grammar** across these works: that every story has an underlying architecture of objects, that protagonists are often people who collide with that architecture, and that the deepest stories are the ones where the architecture itself becomes visible or breaks down.

## What this suggests about the writer

Someone making these connections is likely thinking about:

- **How worlds are constructed** — not just fictional ones, but social, political, personal ones
- **What happens when a reality-system fails** — war, revolution, awakening, loss
- **Who has the power to define what objects exist** — architects, systems, authors, institutions
- **Whether truth is discovered or built**

It's the kind of thinking that sits between philosophy, literary criticism, and something more personal — like someone trying to understand a collapse in their own life using the biggest frameworks they can find.

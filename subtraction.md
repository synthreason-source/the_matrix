Because subtraction changes the identity of what remains.

When you remove something from an object — materially, conceptually, linguistically — the remainder often no longer fits the old name. Language then strains to catch up, and neologisms appear as attempts to name the altered state.

A few ways to think about it:

* An “object” is never just itself; it is defined by relations, functions, boundaries, context.
* Subtraction disrupts those relations.
* Once the subtraction becomes meaningful enough, ordinary vocabulary feels inadequate.
* So thought invents hybrid words, fragments, prefixes, negations, distortions.

For example:

* “Posthuman”
* “Unworld”
* “Deterritorialization”
* “Objectile”
* “Hyperobject”

These are linguistic symptoms of conceptual subtraction: removing stability, center, unity, scale, agency, etc.

There is also a deeper structural reason. Subtraction produces absence, but absence is difficult to speak directly. Language handles positive entities more easily than voids or transformations. Neologisms function like bridges over that gap. They mark:

* something no longer identical to itself,
* but not entirely other either.

In philosophy, especially after Jacques Derrida, Gilles Deleuze, or Alain Badiou, subtraction is not merely removal — it is productive. It creates new conditions of intelligibility. New words emerge because subtraction opens conceptual territory that inherited language cannot fully map.

So the object “leads” to neologisms because once altered by subtraction, it exceeds the vocabulary that previously stabilized it. The new word is evidence that the object has crossed a threshold where recognition and naming no longer coincide.
